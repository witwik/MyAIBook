I Get AI With a Little Help From My Friends\

A Practical Guide for Real People\

By Kenneth Langdon | Version 1.1 | March 2026

A word of warning: the technology in this guide is moving so fast that some of it will be out of date before you finish reading. I would apologise, but by the time I did, the apology would be out of date too. Read quickly. The principles last. The specific model names and prices have a shelf life measured in months.

Pricing accurate as of March 2026. Most tools charge in USD.

Why This Guide Exists

I am sharing this guide with friends, colleagues, and anyone else who wants a straight answer to a simple question: what can I actually do with this stuff?

Not what might be possible in five years. Not what a tech company is promising in a press release. What you can sit down and do, today, on the device in front of you, that will make a practical difference to how you work and live.

I wrote it because I am watching smart, capable people fall into one of three traps.

The first trap is thinking AI means ChatGPT. It does not. ChatGPT is one tool among many. There are tools that are better at writing. Tools that are better at research. Tools that generate music, build websites, edit video, clone voices, and run tasks on your computer while you do something else. There are tools that sit inside your Google apps and tools that run entirely on your own machine with no data leaving your laptop. The landscape in 2026 is vast, most of it is free to try, and almost nobody I talk to has a clear picture of what is out there. This guide gives you that picture.

The second trap is being locked into whatever your employer provides and assuming that is the whole story. If your company gives you Microsoft Copilot and nothing else, that is fine for work. But outside of work, the tools available to you are significantly more capable. If your personal phone is not managed by your employer, you can install Claude, ChatGPT, or any other tool and use it freely for your own purposes. Some employers do require security software on personal devices — I had exactly this at Fujitsu — and if that is your situation, use a device that is not under corporate management, or simply email the results to yourself. The point is that your employer's AI limitations do not have to be yours.

The third trap is refusing to engage on principle. Some people I respect deeply have real concerns about AI and creativity, about whether generated content is authentic, about training data and artist rights, about what it means for jobs. Those are legitimate concerns and I address them directly in Part Four.

But here is what I want to say up front, clearly and without hedging: AI is a tool. It is here to stay. And choosing not to understand it is choosing to be disadvantaged.

Someone you know is using AI to plan a week of family dinners, generate a shopping list, and never have the "what are we eating tonight" argument again. Someone else is photographing their electricity bill and getting a plain-English explanation of what they are actually paying for. A parent is using it to prepare for a GP appointment so they actually ask the right questions instead of walking out and remembering them in the car park.

These are not power users. These are normal people who sat down, tried a prompt, and realised they had been doing things the hard way.

AI does not replace your thinking. It replaces the tedious work that surrounds your thinking.

That is what this guide is for. Not to sell you on AI. Not to hype it. To help you become its master, not its passenger.

How to Use This Guide

If you are sceptical about whether AI is useful at all, start with the three free experiments. Ten minutes. No signup.

If you are mainly here for personal and home use, start with Getting Started, then go to Everyday Life, Using Your Camera, and the Prompt Library.

If you already use AI tools and want to go deeper, jump to whatever interests you in What You Can Do.

If you run a community group or volunteer organisation, see Community Groups.

Whatever else you read, do not skip AI Scams and Deepfakes. It may be the most immediately valuable thing in this guide.

A note on honesty. I use all of these tools. I pay for several of them with my own money. I have no sponsorship deals, affiliate arrangements (with one exception noted in the text), or financial relationships with any of the companies mentioned. Where I prefer one tool over another, I say so and explain why. Where a tool has problems, I say that too. You are getting my honest assessment, not a sales pitch.
